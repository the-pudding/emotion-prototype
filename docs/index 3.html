How are you doing?

A journey through the complexity of emotions.

By Abby VanMuijen with Michelle McGhee

Hi, how are you doing?

Hi there, my name is Abby. I have been doing a lot of learning about FEELINGS these days. Can I show you what I’ve been learning?

Until recently, I would always answer the question “how are you doing?” with one of those options– okay, good, not so good, or busy.

But then that started to change.

I started to experience panic attacks, self harm, depression, and anxiety.

So I got my butt into therapy (where they don't let you off the hook with answers like this...).

My therapist suggested I use a feelings wheel to help improve my "emotional vocabulary."

I could choose words from the wheel to be more specific about how I was feeling and give that feeling a name.

You can choose up to 3.

This kind of specificity of emotional vocabulary is what Lisa Feldman Barrett has coined "emotional granularity." When we can get specific about the emotions we're feeling, we can more easily identify what our body is telling us it needs. Pretty cool, eh?

The other thing I thought was interesting about the wheel was that it organized the feelings by color and category. Although I didn't quite like the colors and categories on the wheels I found...

Colors can have very strong emotional associations - but they're different for everyone.

I started to do this every day - I'd write the words I was feeling and the color I associated with that feeling.

I found a lot of my associations lined up with the colors & character designs from the Pixar movie "Inside Out" - so I studied their process.

It turns out, most scientists agree, there are at least 5 core emotions that track back to the way our bodies intuitively communicate our needs to our caregivers before we have language.

isn’t that FASCINATING?

So I added a new element to my practice - tracking how feelings showed up in my body and facial expressions.

When I feel frustrated, it looks like this:

Take a moment here to pause and notice your body. Start at the top-most part of your body and scan down through your center, out to your edges. Make a mark on the diagram on the right based on what you notice.

I started to notice some patterns in how the colors I was using lined up with how and where the feelings showed up in my body. From there I could track that to some patterns of what my body was trying to ask for.

Our bodies are the source of SO much information, but for lots of reasons, it can be very overwhelming to know what to do with it all.

Do any of the options to the right sound like something your body might enjoy in this moment?

Select up to 5.

Brené Brown says there are as many theories on emotions as there are emotions researchers - which seems right to me.

And while I'm more of an artist than a researcher, this work has helped me very deeply.

I ended up turning all of this into my own emotion wheel, and have used the words, the colors, and the body and needs mapping to build a more visual practice to understand and express how I'm doing each day and what I may be needing to experience more joy (satisfied needs) in my life.

I no longer answer the question “how are you doing?” or understand other people’s responses the same way.

In difficult moments, I have learned how to pause… and ask myself “what word is this? What color is it? Where do I notice it? What is my body telling me it needs?”

When I can identify my feelings, I can identify my needs. When I can identify my needs, I can take care of myself and ask those who care for me for the right kind of help.

And perhaps most important of all, when I can identify my feelings– especially the hard ones– I can more easily treat them as temporary– I can hold them, listen to them and eventually, let them go.

It’s a hard time to be a human these days, but building the skills and practices to tune into our emotional selves can really help.
